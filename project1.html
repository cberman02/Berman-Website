Data

To find agencies and ridership numbers, I took ridership data from the National Transit Database (NTD) for each agency in the country, and identified what agencies implemented partnerships at what time. I also used the American Census Survey (ACS) to find demographic information for each urbanized area (UZA). I linked the UZA demographic data with the UZA the transit agency was associated with in order to confirm that demographic characteristics were not driving ridership growth in agencies with a partnership versus agencies without.

Methodology/Results

While I had initially planned on a standard difference-in-difference specification, the staggered timing forced me to look into a staggered difference-in-difference. This regression technique is susceptible to bias with unbalanced treatment and control groups, as well as to heterogeneous treatment effect, both of which my data exhibited. To combat this, I used interaction-weighted (IW) estimators (Sun and Abraham, "Estimating dynamic treatment effects in event studies with heterogeneous treatment effects", 2021).

This method led to a finding of a 5% increase in transit ridership as a result of these partnerships. However, this increase varies based on the agency. The cohort plot shows the different treatment effect by when the partnership was launched. The results of the regression can be found in the table.
